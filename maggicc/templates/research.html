Synopsis

My research focuses on computer security. During my Ph.D. I studied and made contributions in the field of intrusion detection. More precisely, I developed and tested anomaly-based techniques that mitigate Internet threats by

  1. avoiding their spread via vulnerable web applications,
  2. detecting unexpected activities in the operating system's kernel (sing of malware infections or compromised processes),
  3. dealing with high number of alerts using alert correlation.

In the past, I also did some studies the field of theoretical computer science and contributed with the definition and implementation of a recognizer for rational trace languages.

Current research interests

My research activity in computer security is characterized by a multitude of topics: malware, visualization, social networks, web. However, whenever I stumble upon a new topic or idea, I tend to let it grow into a research project. In the following, I will go through some of the project that I have been working on.


Malware naming conventions inconsistencies

I have been studying the different malware naming schemes adopted by anti-malware software, toward creating a global map of the current knowledge about malware. Malware analysts, and in particular antivirus vendors, never agreed on naming conventions. This leads to confusion and difficulty—more for researchers than for practitioners—for example, when comparing different antivirus engines, when integrating and systematizing known threats, or comparing the classifications given by different detectors. Clearly, solving naming inconsistencies is a very difficult task, as it requires that vendors agree on a unified naming convention. More importantly, solving inconsistencies is impossible without knowing exactly where they are. In this project I took a step back and concentrated on the problem of finding inconsistencies. I leveraged graph-based models of each vendor's naming convention, which I used to define precisely the notion of inconsistency, and to measure quantitatively the overall degree of inconsistency between vendors. More importantly, I proposed a fast algorithm that finds non-trivial (i.e., beyond syntactic differences) inconsistencies. I experimented this technique on four major antivirus vendors and 98,798 real-world malware samples.


Visualization of malicious activity on the Internet

Recently, I made contributions in the fields of security visualization, with the twofold goal of increasing user awareness and providing experts with usable investigation tools: Our tool, called BURN, received the best paper award at VizSec. Manual analysis of security-related events is still a necessity to investigate non-trivial cyber attacks. This task is particularly hard when the events involve slow, stealthy and large-scale activities typical of the modern cybercriminals' strategy. In this regard, visualization tools can effectively help analysts in their investigations. BURN is an interactive visualization tool for displaying autonomous systems exhibiting rogue activity that helps at finding misbehaving networks through visual and interactive exploration. Up to seven values are displayed in a single visual element, while avoiding cumbersome and confusing maps. To this end, animations and alpha channels are leveraged to create simple views that highlight relevant activity patterns. In addition, BURN incorporates a simple algorithm to identify migrations of nefarious services across autonomous systems, which can support, for instance, root-cause analysis and law enforcement investigations.


Automatic shoulder surfing

Given the spread of mobile devices, I have been also studying the extent to which automated shoulder surfing attacks may be used to circumvent software protections, especially against mobile touchscreen devices that expose sensitive information during typing. I arise this concern by presenting an automatic attack against modern touchscreen keyboards. I demonstrate the attack against the Apple iPhone although it can be adapted to other devices (e.g., Android) that employ similar key-magnifying keyboards. The attack processes the stream of frames from a video camera (e.g., surveillance or portable camera) and recognizes keystrokes online, in a fraction of the time needed to perform the same task by direct observation or offline analysis of a recorded video, which can be unfeasible for large amount of data. The attack detects, tracks, and rectifies the target touchscreen, thus following the device or camera's movements and eliminating possible perspective distortions and rotations In real-world settings, the attack can automatically recognize up to 97.07 percent of the keystrokes (91.03 on average), with 1.15 percent of errors (3.16 on average) at a speed ranging from 37 to 51 keystrokes per minute.
